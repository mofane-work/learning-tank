One-time payments: the PaymentIntent lifecycle

A single payment isn't one event — it's an object that moves through a series of states. Follow one from "I owe $20" all the way to money in your bank.

Lesson 0004 ~8 min Money flow: one-time

Why a payment needs its own object

A card payment isn't instantaneous or guaranteed. It may need the bank's authentication, it may be retried, it may take days (for some methods), and it can fail. So Stripe models a payment as a PaymentIntent — an object that, in Stripe's words, "tracks a payment from creation through checkout, and triggers additional authentication steps when required." The PaymentIntent is the payment; its status tells you where it's got to.

The three actors

PaymentIntent  ── the payment itself, with an amount and a status
   + PaymentMethod  ── the card (or other method) that will pay it
   ──────────────
   = Charge         ── one attempt to actually move the money

You create a PaymentIntent for an amount, attach a PaymentMethod, and confirm it. Confirming produces a Charge — the record of one attempt. If a first attempt fails and the customer retries, you can get more than one Charge on the same PaymentIntent: Stripe notes "a PaymentIntent might have more than one Charge object… if there were multiple payment attempts." The PaymentIntent is the payment; each Charge is an attempt at it.

The state machine

A PaymentIntent walks through a defined set of statuses. You rarely drive all of these by hand — a tool like Checkout does it for you — but recognising them tells you exactly where a payment is stuck:

requires_payment_method   no card attached yet (or the last one failed)
        │
        ▼
requires_confirmation     card attached, ready to confirm
        │
        ▼
requires_action           needs the customer to authenticate (e.g. 3D Secure)
        │
        ▼
processing                submitted; some methods take a while
        │
        ▼
succeeded                 done — the funds are yours to fulfil against

Straight from the docs, two anchors worth memorising:

  • Start: "After you create the PaymentIntent, its status is requires_payment_method until you attach a payment method."
  • End: "A PaymentIntent with a succeeded status means that the corresponding payment flow is complete. The funds are now in your account and you can confidently fulfil the order."

Two side exits: canceled (you call it off before it processes — "cancellation invalidates the PaymentIntent for future payment attempts, releases any held funds, and can't be undone") and requires_capture, which appears only if you authorise funds now and capture them later (e.g. hold on a hotel booking, charge at checkout).

Don't fulfil before succeeded. The single most common payments bug is shipping the goods when the customer clicks "pay" rather than when the PaymentIntent actually reaches succeeded. A payment in requires_action or processing has not paid yet. Wait for succeeded — ideally via the payment_intent.succeeded webhook from Lesson 1.

Authentication (SCA), conceptually

Sometimes the bank wants the customer to prove it's really them — a fingerprint, a banking-app tap, a code. That's Strong Customer Authentication, required for many European cards. In the model it's just the requires_action state: the PaymentIntent pauses, the customer authenticates, and it continues. You don't implement the cryptography — you just handle the fact that a payment can need a step in the middle. This is a big reason the PaymentIntent exists rather than a single "charge this card" call.

From Charge to cash in your bank

A succeeded payment isn't money in your bank account yet — it's money in your Stripe balance. The tail of the flow:

Charge ──> Balance transaction ──> Payout
(the attempt)  (the +amount, −fee     (Stripe sends your
               on your Stripe balance)  balance to your bank)
  • A balance transaction records the gross amount, Stripe's fee, and the net that lands on your balance.
  • A Payout is Stripe moving your accumulated balance to your real bank account, on a schedule (often daily). Refunds and disputes flow back through the same balance.

So "the customer paid" and "the money is in my bank" are two distinct moments, with your Stripe balance in between.
