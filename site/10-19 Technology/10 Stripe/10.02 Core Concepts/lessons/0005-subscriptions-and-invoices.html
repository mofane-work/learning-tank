Recurring billing: Subscriptions and Invoices

Recurring revenue is the one-time payment machinery on a timer. A Subscription ties a Customer to a Price; each cycle it raises an Invoice that pays itself.

Lesson 0005 ~8 min Money flow: recurring

What a Subscription connects

A Subscription is the object that puts billing on autopilot. It connects two things you already know:

Subscription (sub_...)
   ├─ a Customer        who to bill, and which saved card to use
   └─ Subscription item(s) ── each points at a recurring Price (× quantity)

Each line in a subscription is a subscription item = one recurring Price with a quantity. Most subscriptions have a single item ("1 × Pro Plan at $20/month"); a subscription with several items is how you bill a base plan plus add-ons on one cycle.

It reuses the parts you've met. The Customer (Lesson 3), the recurring Price (Lesson 2), the saved PaymentMethod (Lesson 3) — a Subscription just binds them together and adds a clock. Nothing new about how money moves; only when.

The billing cycle and the Invoice

At the start of each billing period the Subscription raises an Invoice — an itemised bill for that period. Straight from Stripe: "Whenever a payment is due for a subscription, Stripe generates an invoice and a PaymentIntent," and "the subscription continues to generate invoices each billing period." The Invoice carries line items (one per subscription item, plus any one-off charges or prorations), and Stripe then tries to pay it automatically with the customer's default card.

Billing date arrives
        │
        ▼
Stripe creates an Invoice   (line items for this cycle)
        │
        ▼
Invoice is finalised        (becomes "open" — the amount is locked in)
        │
        ▼
Stripe creates a PaymentIntent and charges the default card
        │
        ├─ paid       ──> Invoice "paid"; service continues
        └─ failed     ──> Invoice "open"; retries / dunning begin

That bottom step is the whole point: an Invoice is paid by a PaymentIntent — the very object from Lesson 4. Recurring billing doesn't invent a new way to move money; it auto-generates the same one-time payment flow on schedule. The map's two money flows converge here.

The Subscription's status tells you the health of the relationship

A Subscription carries a status reflecting whether billing is current:

  • trialing — in a free trial; no charge yet.
  • active — paid and current. The normal happy state.
  • past_due — a payment failed; Stripe is retrying (this retry process is called dunning).
  • canceled / unpaid — ended, or given up on after retries.

Your app typically grants or revokes access based on this status — usually by listening for the subscription's update webhooks rather than checking on every request.

Where Prorations fit. When a subscriber upgrades, downgrades, or changes quantity mid-cycle, Stripe adds proration line items to the next Invoice — a credit for unused time and a charge for the new rate. That mechanism is its own topic: 10.01 Prorations. Here, just note that those adjustments ride on the ordinary Invoice you've now seen.

Invoices exist outside subscriptions too

An Invoice is a general "itemised bill we'll try to collect" object. Subscriptions are the most common source of invoices, but you can also create a standalone invoice for a one-off bill (add invoice items, finalise, send). Same object, same paid-by-a-PaymentIntent ending — which is why learning it once pays off twice.
