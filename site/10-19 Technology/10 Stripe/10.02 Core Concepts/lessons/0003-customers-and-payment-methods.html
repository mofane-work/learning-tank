Customers and payment methods

The Customer is the hub the rest of the graph hangs off. Get this object right and saved cards, subscriptions, and invoices all have a home.

Lesson 0003 ~6 min The parties

The Customer is a hub, not just a contact

A Customer object represents the person or business you charge. It holds the obvious things — email, name, address — but its real job is to be the anchor that everything else attaches to:

Customer (cus_...)
   ├─ PaymentMethod(s)     saved cards / bank accounts
   ├─ default payment method   which one to charge automatically
   ├─ Subscription(s)      ongoing recurring billing
   ├─ Invoice(s)           bills raised against them
   └─ a credit balance     money owed to/from them

You can take a one-off payment without ever creating a Customer. But the moment you want to charge someone again — a second purchase, a subscription, a saved card — you need a Customer to hang it on. That's the dividing line: one-and-done vs. a relationship.

PaymentMethod: a card, abstracted

A PaymentMethod represents how a customer pays — a card, a bank account, a wallet like Apple Pay, a regional method like iDEAL or SEPA. They all become the same kind of object so the rest of Stripe can treat "a way to pay" uniformly.

Crucially, your servers never see the raw card number. The customer's card details go straight from their browser to Stripe, which returns a PaymentMethod object you can safely store and reference by ID. That's the core of how Stripe keeps you out of PCI scope.

Attaching matters. A PaymentMethod can exist on its own, but to reuse it you attach it to a Customer. An attached PaymentMethod is one you can charge later without the customer re-entering anything. An unattached one is good for a single payment and then forgotten.

Saving a card for later: the SetupIntent

There are two reasons to collect a card, and Stripe gives each its own object:

  • Charge now → a PaymentIntent (Lesson 4). Collect the card and move money in one flow.
  • Save now, charge later → a SetupIntent. Collect and validate a card — including any bank authentication — without charging it, so it's ready for future use.

A SetupIntent is what you use before a free trial that will bill later, or to let a customer add a card to their account "on file". It runs the same authentication a real payment would, so the saved card won't surprise you with a decline the first time you actually charge it. Think of it as a PaymentIntent for zero dollars whose only goal is a reusable PaymentMethod.

Why reuse one Customer

It's tempting to create a fresh Customer for every transaction. Don't. One Customer per real-world person or business means:

  • Saved cards work — the next purchase is one click because the PaymentMethod is already attached.
  • Subscriptions have somewhere to live and a default card to bill.
  • Invoices, receipts, and lifetime value all roll up to one record instead of scattering.

The Customer is the thread that turns isolated payments into an account.
