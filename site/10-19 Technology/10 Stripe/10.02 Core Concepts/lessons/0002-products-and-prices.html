Products and Prices

The catalogue half of Stripe, and the one most people get tangled in. A Product is what you sell. A Price is how much and how often. Keeping them apart is the whole lesson.

Lesson 0002 ~7 min The catalogue

Two objects, one clean split

Stripe deliberately splits your catalogue into two objects:

  • A Product describes what you offer — a name and description, nothing about money. In Stripe's words, "Products define what your business offers, whether that's goods or services."
  • A Price describes how much and how often to charge for that product. Stripe: "Prices define how much and how often to charge for products."

So "Pro Plan" is a Product. "$20 per month" is a Price on that product. The customer's receipt shows the product's name and description; only the numbers come from the Price.

Why separate them at all? Because the thing you sell is stable, but how you charge for it changes constantly — different currencies, monthly vs yearly, a promotional rate, a regional discount. Splitting them lets all those variations share one product identity instead of duplicating it.

One Product, many Prices

A single Product can carry many Prices at once. Stripe: "Products can use multiple prices to define different pricing options. The prices share the product description… only the pricing differs." A typical "Pro Plan" product might hold:

Product: "Pro Plan"
   ├─ Price: $20 / month        (USD, recurring monthly)
   ├─ Price: $200 / year        (USD, recurring yearly — two months free)
   ├─ Price: €19 / month        (EUR, recurring monthly)
   └─ Price: $25 / month        (USD, recurring monthly — legacy, now archived)

The customer picks a Price at checkout. The Product just ties them together so reporting, receipts, and your catalogue all agree on what was sold.

Recurring vs one-time

The most important field on a Price is whether it recurs:

  • A recurring Price has an interval — month, year, week, day. It's what a Subscription charges on a cycle (Lesson 5).
  • A one-time Price has no interval. It's charged once — a single sale via a PaymentIntent (Lesson 4).

The same Product can have both: a "Design Course" product might have a one-time $99 Price (buy it outright) and a $12/month recurring Price (pay monthly). One catalogue entry, two ways to pay.

Where the money amount lives. A Price stores its amount in the currency's smallest unit — unit_amount: 2000 means $20.00, not $2000. Stripe counts in cents (or the equivalent) to avoid rounding errors. A frequent source of "why did I charge 100× too much" bugs.

Prices are immutable — you don't edit them, you replace them

You cannot change the amount on an existing Price. From Stripe's docs: "Instead of changing the unit_amount on the existing price, you need to create a new price… to make sure that we keep the existing price as an immutable record of past transactions." To raise your price you:

  1. Create a new Price ($25/month) on the same Product.
  2. Archive the old one by setting active = false so it can't be picked for new sales.

Existing subscriptions keep pointing at the old Price until you move them. This is what keeps a two-year-old invoice honest: the Price it referenced still says exactly what was charged then. (Moving a subscriber from the old Price to the new one mid-cycle is precisely what 10.01 Prorations is about.)

Beyond a flat amount

A Price can encode more than one number when you need it: tiers (volume or graduated pricing — cheaper per unit as quantity grows), usage-based billing (charge by metered consumption), and tax behaviour (whether the amount is tax-inclusive). You don't need these to start — just know the Price object is where that complexity lives, not the Product.
