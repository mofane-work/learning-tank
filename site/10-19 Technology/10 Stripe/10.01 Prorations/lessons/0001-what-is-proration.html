What is a proration?

When a subscription changes mid-cycle, Stripe splits the bill into a credit and a charge. This is the one idea everything else rests on.

Lesson 0001 ~5 min Core mechanics

The one idea

A customer is paying for a subscription in advance — say $10/month, billed on the 1st. Halfway through the month they upgrade to a $20/month plan. They have already paid for a full month of the cheaper plan, but they'll only use half of it. And they now want the pricier plan for the rest of the month, which they haven't paid for.

A proration is how Stripe squares that up. It does two things at once:

  • Credits the customer for the time they paid for but won't use on the old price.
  • Charges the customer for the time remaining on the new price.

Stripe's own words: a proration is calculated from "the cost of the subscription before and after the change" and the point in the billing period when the change happens (Stripe Billing — Prorations).

Where it shows up: two line items

A proration isn't a single mysterious number. It appears as two separate line items, normally on the customer's next invoice:

  • A negative line — e.g. "Unused time on $10 plan" — the credit.
  • A positive line — e.g. "Remaining time on $20 plan" — the charge.

Because they're two lines, you can always see why a customer was charged what they were. The amount they actually owe for the change is simply the two added together.

A worked example

Take the upgrade above, with the change landing exactly halfway through the month:

Old plan: $10/month  →  half a month unused  =  −$5.00  (credit)
New plan: $20/month  →  half a month remaining =  +$10.00 (charge)
                                          ─────────────────────
                            Net effect of the change =  +$5.00

So the upgrade adds a net $5.00 for the rest of this period. From next month onward, the customer simply pays the full $20 on their normal billing date. This matches Stripe's documented example exactly: upgrading a $10 plan to a $20 plan halfway through bills "an additional 5 USD: −5 USD for unused time on the initial price, and 10 USD for the remaining time on the new price."

A downgrade just flips the signs. Drop from $20 to $10 mid-month and the credit for unused expensive time outweighs the charge for cheap remaining time — the proration nets negative. Stripe calls these credit prorations. What happens to a negative balance is its own topic (Lesson 4).

The proration date

To split the period into "used" and "remaining", Stripe needs an exact instant — the proration date. Stripe "prorates to the second", so the credit and charge are computed from precisely when the change is applied, not rounded to the nearest day. That precision matters later when you want to preview an amount and have it still be correct when you apply the change (Lesson 3).

One catch to carry forward

Calculating a proration and billing it are two different things. By default Stripe creates the proration line items but does not charge the customer immediately — the amount waits on the next invoice. Stripe is explicit: "Negative prorations aren't automatically refunded and positive prorations aren't immediately billed." Controlling that timing is exactly what proration_behavior does, which is Lesson 2.
