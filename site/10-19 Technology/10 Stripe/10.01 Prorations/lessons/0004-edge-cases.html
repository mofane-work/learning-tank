Learning Tank Technology Stripe Prorations 0004

Edge cases & gotchas

The core model is simple. These are the four places it bites people in production.

Lesson 0004 ~7 min Gotchas

You've got the model (L1), the controls (L2), and the preview (L3). This final lesson is a tour of where prorations behave in ways that surprise a product or finance reader.

1. The billing cycle anchor

The billing cycle anchor is "the reference point that aligns future billing period dates" — it fixes which day of the month (or week, or year) the customer is billed on. Prorations are always measured against the period this anchor defines.

The gotcha is moving it. If you reset the anchor to now, "Stripe immediately sends an invoice." And critically: "Enable proration to credit the customer for any days already paid in the previous period. Disabling proration might result in overcharging your customer." So resetting an anchor with proration_behavior=none can make a customer pay twice for the same days.

2. Free trials

A free trial "automatically set[s] the billing cycle anchor to the trial end date." That's usually what you want — but it can leave a partial period that Stripe then prorates. The docs warn this "might result in a prorated invoice following the trial period." Their example: a customer already paid through July 23, so a "free" period from a new trial "only applies to July 24 through July 31."

The standard move: when you're using a trial purely to shift someone's billing date, set proration_behavior=none, "because the length of the trial period accounts for the portion already paid." The trial absorbs the partial period instead of a proration doing it.

3. Multiple changes in one period

Prorations stack. Change a plan twice before the next invoice and you don't get one tidy adjustment — you get a proration for each change, each crediting the state before it and charging the state after. Upgrade then downgrade in the same week and the next invoice carries several credit and charge lines that only make sense read in order. For finance, the lesson is: don't expect one line per customer per period; expect one pair per change.

4. Negative prorations and credit balance

When a proration nets negative (a downgrade, a cancellation mid-period), the money doesn't come back as a refund. Stripe is blunt: "Negative prorations aren't automatically refunded." Instead the credit sits on the customer's account as credit balance and is applied against their future invoices, quietly reducing what they owe next time.

So a downgrade often produces no immediate visible event — no refund, no charge — just a credit that surfaces weeks later as a smaller invoice. If a customer says "I downgraded and got nothing back," this is usually why.

And one boundary: proration "only appl[ies] to charges that occur ahead of the billing period. Usage-based billing isn't subject to proration." Metered usage is billed for what was actually used, so there's nothing to prorate — don't expect proration lines for usage items.
